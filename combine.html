BUTTERFLY SIGHTINGS IN AUSTRALIA

Year 2013-2023

Recording butterfly sightings in Australia is crucial for monitoring biodiversity and assessing the health of ecosystems. Butterflies are sensitive indicators of environmental changes, such as climate shifts and habitat loss. Their presence or absence can signal broader ecological trends and help scientists make informed conservation decisions. Citizen scientists' contributions, such as the data displayed in this visualisation, enhance our understanding of local and regional species distributions, aiding in the protection of these delicate and beautiful insects and the ecosystems they inhabit.

GEOGRAPHIC SIGHTINGS


Geographic Distribution

The visualisation on the left showcases the geographic distribution of butterfly sightings across Australia from year 2013-2023. Analysis of the visualisation reveals that butterfly sightings are most common in costal regions of Australia.

This could be attributed to:

1. Coastal regions offering a more favourable temperature range for butterfly activity.

2. More abundant plants are often more abundant in coastal regions due to the milder climate and higher rainfall.

3. Coastal areas tend to have a greater variety of habitats, including forests, wetlands, and open spaces, which provide diverse environments and food sources for butterflies.

4. Costal areas are more populated than other regions of Australia, resulting in more recorded sightings from citizen scientists.

Trends in sightings based on monthes of the year

Analysing the histogram on the right reveals that:

1. March documented the highest number of sightings.

2. August documented the lowest number of sightings.

3. Comparable sightings were recorded in October, November and December.

These observations are in line with scientific estimations based on the seasonal climate, life cycle patterns, migration, and floral availability, which are likely to cause variation in butterfly sightings in Australia throughout the year.

TRENDS IN SIGHTINGS (2/2)

DATA ANALYSIS




Sightings data analysis

The donut chart on the left-hand side illustrates several key insights regarding butterfly sightings in Australia. Firstly, Queensland stands out as the region with the highest number of total butterfly sightings, indicating its prevalence as a habitat for these insects. Following Queensland, the order of most to least sightings goes as follows: New South Wales, Victoria, Tasmania, Australian Capital Territory, South Australia, Western Australia, and the Northern Territory with the fewest recorded sightings. Meanwhile, the accompanying bar chart on the right-hand side delves into the family distribution of these sightings. Nymphalidae emerges as the most frequently observed butterfly family, followed by Pieridae, Lycarnidae, and Papilionidae. Making Hesperiidae the least commonly sighted family of butterflies in the region.
