BUTTERFLY SIGHTINGS IN AUSTRALIA Year 2013-2023

Recording butterfly sightings in Australia is crucial for monitoring biodiversity and assessing the health of ecosystems. Butterflies are sensitive indicators of environmental changes, such as climate shifts and habitat loss. Their presence or absence can signal broader ecological trends and help scientists make informed conservation decisions.


Citizen scientists' contributions, such as the data displayed in this visualisation, enhance our understanding of local and regional species distributions, aiding in the protection of these delicate and beautiful insects and the ecosystems they inhabit.

GEOGRAPHIC SIGHTINGS IN AUSTRALIA


Geographic Distribution

The visualisation on the left showcases the geographic distribution of butterfly sightings across Australia from year 2013-2023. Analysis of the visualisation reveals that butterfly sightings are most common in costal regions of Australia.

This could be attributed to:
1. Coastal regions offering a more favourable temperature range for butterfly activity.
2. Coastal areas tend to have a greater variety of habitats for butterflies.
3. Costal areas are more populated than other regions, hence more recorded sightings from citizens.

TRENDS IN SIGHTINGS OVER YEARS


Trends in sightings across
2013-2023

This dot-map maps the sightings across 2013-2023, revealing that:

1. The total number of sightings is steadily increasing across the years.
2. This was revealed to be true for all family of butterflies when the Family filter is applied.
3. The number of sightings in individual encounters has also increased over the years, with larger circles (indicating a greater number of sightings) observed more frequently in recent years.
4. This was similarly revealed to be true for all family of butterflies when the Family filter is applied.

TRENDS IN SIGHTINGS OVER MONTHS


Trends in sightings based on months of the year

Analysing the histogram on the right reveals that:
1. March documented the highest number of sightings.
2. August documented the lowest number of sightings.
3. Comparable sightings were recorded in October, November and December.
These observations are in line with scientific estimation based on the seasonal climate, life cycle patterns, migration, and floral availability in Australia throughout the year.

SIGHTINGS CATAGORISED BASED ON STATE AND FAMILY

Analysis of the donut chart, reveals that:

1. Sightings are most common in Queensland, with the highest recorded number of total butterfly sightings.
2. Followed by New South Wales, Victoria, Tasmania, Australian Capital Territory, South Australia, Western Australia and with the least number of sightings recorded in the Northern Territory.

Analysis of the bar chart, reveals that:

1. Of the recorded betterflies, Nymphalidae is the most common family of butterfly.
2. Followed by Pieridae, Lycarnidae, Papilionidae, and with Hesperiidae being the least commonly sighted family of butterflies.
